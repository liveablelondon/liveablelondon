Exploring the spatial distribution of neighbourhood typologies in London

through an interactive mapping tool

Every year, 200,000 people move from different parts of the UK to live in London. On top of this, people from across the world choose to make London their home too. Whether it be for work, education, business or living closer to family and friends, it is undeniable that London offers a plethora of opportunities and reasons to relocate for diverse groups of people.

The myriad of options presented by London can be captivating, yet daunting at the same time. Something seemingly as straightforward as choosing where to live (if you’re in a smaller city or town) is complicated by the fact that London is huge and comprises many distinct neighbourhoods that suit the needs of particular groups of people. To simplify the process and aid your house-hunting adventures, this tool visualises the trade-offs between different liveability factors for locations across London

What are the neighbourhood factors that are important to you?

Each of you would have different sets of neighbourhood aspects you consider are more important when choosing an area to live in.

We have identified 6 main liveability factors to investigate on this tool, which are housing, environment, green access, safety, education, and transportation.

Here, we introduce each factor and how we quantify the level achieved in each area.

Housing

Median house price as indicator

Housing Metric

The housing metric was created by considering the 2014 median house price data across all London wards. This dataset was obtained from the London Ward Profiles and Atlas, which is provided by the Greater London Authority. Median house price was considered as it gives a better understanding of house prices compared to the mean, which includes data for exceptionally expensive sales that might not be representative of the as well. Although rental data is not included within this metric, house prices do give a good indication on the corresponding rental prices for the property.

Pollution Metric

With increased discussion around London’s air quality over the last few years, air quality is a key focus of the Mayor of London’s 2018 Environment Strategy. Particulate matter with a diameter of less than 2.5 micrometres (PM2.5) can be found in air samples collected from across London. Open Space

Green/open spaces as indicators

Open Space Metric

Green and open spaces were used as a proxy to calculate the neighbourhood metric. The rationale for this lies in the fact that the opportunities open and green spaces provide for interaction, leisure and activities are essential factors in determining what makes a neighbourhood liveable. To create this metric, data was extracted from the 2013 London Ward Well-Being Scores, under the column “Homes with access to open space & nature, and % greenspace”. Wards are given a score that could be positive and negative, with positive scores indicating a higher probability that the population on average will experience better well-being for that corresponding metric. A score of 0 means that the ward has a score that tallies with the average value in England and Wales for that corresponding indicator.

Safety

Residential burglary as indicator

Safety Metric

The safety metric was created using Recorded Crime data provided by the London Metropolitan Police. In particular, 2009 crime data aggregated at the London Ward level was used. Instead of analysing all crime which could skew the data towards high-traffic, touristy areas around Westminster and central London, crimes targeting homes was specifically looked at. Thus, residential burglaries was used as an indicator for safety. Wards with lower levels of home burglaries will therefore be recognised as safer in our model.

Education

Avg. GCSE scores as indicator

Education Metric

To compute the education metric, average GCSE scores were used. Average GCSE scores give important information regarding the educational performance of students living in a particular ward, along with potentially how well the local catchment performs in education. To create this metric, data was extracted from the 2013 London Ward Well-Being Scores, under the column “Average GCSE scores”. Wards are given a score that could be positive and negative, with positive scores indicating a higher probability that the population on average will experience better education scores. A score of 0 means that the ward has an educational standard that corresponds with the average of England and Wales.

Transportaion

PTAL scores as indicator

Transportation Metric

The transportation metric is informed by the calculated Public Transport Accessibility Level (PTAL) score, aggregated at ward level. Ranging from a value of 1 to 6, PTAL scores indicate how well connected and accessible a place is with regards to public transport. A score of 1 indicates that the corresponding ward has extremely poor access to public transport connections whereas a score of 6 indicates that the ward is very well connected to various public transport options in London.

Explore which ward has high scores for the factors you have identified as your priorities:

Data for the 6 main liveability factors were compiled and aggregated at ward level. This provides a good indication of the characteristic of a particular ward, in terms of its liveability.

Instead of displaying the raw values for each ward, the map here demonstrates how each ward performs in each of these indicators, relative to all London wards by taking into account its percentile for each metric. This allows you to compare how certain wards fare in particular metrics, whilst also comparing across different metrics.

Given that the distribution for all metrics (log value of housing was taken) were close to normally distributed, this method will also give you an indication of how a particular ward fares, within the distribution.

If you are still unsure, don't fret! We have created general profiles you may identify yourself to so that we can suggest some areas for you to explore.



FIRST TIME RENTERS/STUDENTS

Limited budget, currently saving up for a home. Typically want to stay around their friends and ideally live somewhere that is close to the action and bustle of city life.
YOUNG FAMILIES

Couples that have recently started a young family, with children below 10 years of age. Facing a big challenge to juggle between career and childcare. Access to transport isn’t too much of a worry given that they own cars for their daily commute.

OLDER FAMILIES

Families with children in their teens. Children are at the age where they can start taking care of themselves, so they’ll ideally rely on public transport to get around.
PROFESSIONAL COUPLES

Career oriented, using the home as a stepping stone for their eventual dream house. This group has a firm grasp on financial knowledge and believe that their homes are an investment asset.

SINGLE INCOME OWNERS

Single parents or bachelor*ettes looking for a place to call home. Access to employment and transport accessibility are main determinant factors for these groups. along with house prices.
EMPTY NESTERS AND RETIREES

Retirees and parents whose children have moved out of their houses. An ideal environment is highly sought after for retirement, coupled with good access to health facilities. Transport accessibility is appreciated too, as it reduces the need for the elderly to drive around. Preferably in more quiet parts of the city.
